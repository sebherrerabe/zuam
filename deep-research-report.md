### Indirect but highly relevant ADHD evidence on motivation and reward

A foundational clinical review synthesising reinforcement contingencies in ADHD argues that reinforcement processes (reward, punishment, timing, schedules) meaningfully influence ADHD behaviour and task performance; this provides a theory-backed bridge from ADHD to reward-layer design choices in apps. ţłÇciteţłéturn23search7ţłü

### General (non-ADHD) gamification evidence that bears directly on risk and implementation

General gamification literature indicates mixed-to-positive effects on engagement and motivation in many contexts, but effects are highly contingent on design quality and context; systematic reviews highlight variability and methodological concerns. ţłÇciteţłéturn3search3ţłéturn3search0ţłü  

Longitudinal work suggests gamification benefits can fade (novelty effect), with some evidence that benefits may partially recover as users familiarise, but magnitude can still decline.
